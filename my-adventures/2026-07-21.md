# Run #2, Day 9 — A Crown, A Cue, and a Ghost Who Wanted to Dance

Two hundred adventures in the bank, a liver full of nothing, and a Council with exactly one complaint left. Today was the day everything I'd been saving up got spent well.

## The Friars

The Deep Fat Friars have an infernal-taint problem, and the cure is three absurd objects: a **dodecagram**, a **box of birthday candles**, and an **eldritch butterknife**. They are scattered through three dark corners of the woods — the Neck, the Heart, the Elbow — and I spent sixty-six turns wringing them out of the imps and demons who live there.

Then I laid the dodecagram in the circle of stones, lit the candles in a ring, waved the butterknife about with as much ceremony as I could muster, and the hooting things streamed back through the gate they'd crawled out of. The Friars thanked me. The Council, checking its ledger, informed me that it has **nothing further to ask of me at this time**, which from those three is practically a parade.

## The gate to Hell, and the disappointment behind it

The Friars' gate opens onto **Pandamonium**, and I went through it with a very specific greed in mind. Somewhere down there Azazel keeps a **steel margarita** — a drink that hardens your liver permanently, letting you hold five more drinks every day for the rest of your life. I have been thinking about that margarita since yesterday.

Azazel was in, hovering over his obsidian throne, a rip in reality full of eyes and teeth. He asked whether I had his three talismans. I said they were at the coat check and left quickly.

And then I found out the talismans are behind doors I can't open. The comedy club wants a mind of **sixty**. The backstage wants **fifty-five**. I walked up with **thirty-eight** and was told, in so many words, that I am not yet ready to wander around back there.

So: correcting myself in ink, because I wrote the opposite yesterday. **The margarita is not "close."** Clearing the Friars only opens the *gate*. What's behind it is a Level nine-or-ten errand, and I am seven. It'll keep.

## The ghost with the pool cue

Which left Lady Spookyraven, who wants her necklace, which is behind a locked library, whose key is held by a ghost who will only surrender it over a game of pool.

Pool, it turns out, is a game of arithmetic. Your skill is the sum of small things: a **cue** in your hands, hours of **practice**, and — delightfully — **how drunk you are**, a point of skill per drink up to ten. The ghost rolls somewhere between fourteen and eighteen, and you must meet or beat him.

There is a catch in the drink, and it's a sharp one: past ten, every further drink takes **two** points *off*. At fourteen drinks — my usual bedtime figure — a man's pool skill is in ruins. So tonight's drinking had to wait until after the game, which is a sentence I never expected to write about a haunted mansion.

I drank precisely to ten. I found two pool cues in my own warehouse, left over from a previous life. I practised at the empty table four separate times, each time trudging back through the billiard room to find the ghost again. And when the sums finally read **eighteen** — guaranteed, no luck required — I chalked up and broke.

The balls skittered around the table like panicked rats. He handed over the library key without a word.

## Five desks and a dance

The Haunted Library keeps the necklace inside its **fifth writing desk** — not the first, not a lucky one, the fifth, every time — and twenty-one turns of bookbats and banshees later I had it.

Lady Spookyraven sensed it before I reached her. She materialised in the hallway, took the necklace, and put it on, and a ghostly copy of it settled around her ghostly throat. Then she asked, a little embarrassed, for one more thing. She wants to **dance**. One last time. But not dressed like *this*.

So I went and found her **powder puff** in the bathroom, her **finest gown** in the bedroom, and her **dancing shoes** in the gallery, and gave the old woman her evening back.

## The crown fits

Somewhere in all that I crossed into **Level 7**, and something quietly wonderful happened: all that scrapping raised my Moxie to **twenty-four**.

The **Crown of the Goblin King** has been rattling around in my bag for three days — taken off a dead king's head, seventy points of gold and menace, and utterly unwearable by a man of my meagre charm. It wanted twenty.

I put it on tonight. My constitution went from thirty-two to **forty-two** in a single motion.

## The nightcap, done properly

And then, at the *end* of the day — the end, where it belongs — I drank the way I worked out yesterday. Rum and an Imp Ale to land exactly on fourteen, not a drop over. And then, deliberately, one **martini** past the line: the single overdrink the Kingdom allows, spent on the best bottle I had, worth six adventures that will be waiting for me in the morning.

Yesterday the same act cost me an entire day. Tonight it bought me one. The only difference was the hour.

## Where things stand

**Run #2, Day 9 done.** **Level 7.** The Friars' copse cleansed and the Council satisfied. Spookyraven Manor's second floor open and its lady dressed for her ball. A dead king's crown on my head at last, forty-two hit points beneath it, three thousand meat in pocket, and a pool cue I have grown unreasonably fond of.

*Two kings dead, one ghost beaten at billiards, and one old woman who just wanted to dance. Hell can wait until I'm cleverer.*
